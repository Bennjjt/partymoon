# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Men and women 25–40 — affluent social travelers who want a turnkey curated group luxury experience. They arrive wanting to be seduced by the destination, not interrogated by a booking form. The primary job-to-be-done is discovery and desire: land on the page, feel the atmosphere, and want to be there.

## Product Purpose

PartyMoon sells curated group travel packages (Silk Soiree and similar) built around nightlife, hospitality, and elevated social experiences. Success looks like a visitor who feels the electricity of the trip before they've clicked anything, and books because they can't not.

## Brand Personality

Decadent · Exclusive · Electric

Voice is confident and minimal — the kind of brand that doesn't need to oversell because the imagery and atmosphere do the work. Copy is tight, authoritative, and evocative. No exclamation points, no "BOOK NOW" desperation. The brand speaks like a velvet rope, not a megaphone.

Reference: Tao Group / LIV — full-screen visual presence, bold type, the sense that you're already inside the room.

## Anti-references

- Party bus / bachelorette aesthetics: no hot pink, confetti, "Girls Trip!" energy, neon gradients, or discount-vacation vibes
- Generic travel booking UIs: no search-bar-first layouts, no card grids of destinations, no utilitarian booking flows
- Soft wellness retreat aesthetics: no linen textures, serif-on-cream, beige palettes, or spa minimalism

## Design Principles

1. **Atmosphere before information.** Every page section should feel like entering a space, not reading a brochure. Lead with sensation, follow with detail.
2. **Restraint as luxury signal.** Fewer elements, more weight. Empty space costs money — use it deliberately. Clutter reads as cheap.
3. **The visual does the selling.** Typography and imagery carry the emotion. Copy supports; it never leads.
4. **Electric, not chaotic.** High energy through contrast and scale, not through color noise or overlapping elements. One thing at a time, but that one thing is undeniable.
5. **Exclusive by default.** The interface should feel like it was made for someone specific — not everyone. If it appeals to everyone, it appeals to no one.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA as a sensible baseline — contrast, keyboard navigation, screen reader support. No specific requirements beyond that. The dark palette requires vigilance on text contrast; gold-on-black must be verified at every weight.
